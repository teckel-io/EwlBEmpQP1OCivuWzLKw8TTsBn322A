Benedict — User Guide & Best Practices

Benedict is your voice compliance assistant. He listens (you can type, too), fills in your forms, shows them on screen, and emails the finished paperwork when you’re done. He’s quick, and a few simple habits will keep every session accurate, fast, and as inexpensive as possible.

Getting started

Open your practice’s Benedict page in your browser. You’ll see two things side by side:

  • On the left: the Benedict voice widget — your way to talk to him.
  • On the right: the form viewer (currently empty). Your session cost appears at the top once the session ends.

To start, click the Benedict widget once and allow microphone access if your browser asks. Then just speak — there’s no special wake word. Try “hi Benedict”, “I want to fill in a form”, or “open the Crown consent.”

Your access code

Benedict will first ask for your six-digit access code. Open the teckel App on your phone, navigate to your Account with your teckel credit (tc) balance, tap “Generate Access Code” (a popup will appear at the bottom of your phone screen with your temporary six digit PIN), and read it out loud to Benedict (or type it). PIN codes are single-use and expire after a short while — that’s what keeps your practice’s records locked to your team.

Prefer to type?

The widget accepts typed messages during a voice call as well as speech. Everything in this guide works either way — typing is especially handy for email addresses, serial numbers and access codes.

A typical session, end to end

  1. Greet Benedict. “Hi Benedict” or “I want to work on a form.” He’ll respond and ask what you’d like to do.
  2. Find your form. Either say a phrase from the form’s title (“Crown consent”, “cleaning specification”, “patient survey”) and Benedict will search the catalogue, OR ask “what am I working on?” / “what forms are available?” to hear the lists.
  3. Start filling. Once Benedict has the form, he’ll walk through it question-by-question — OR you can volunteer many answers in one breath. Volunteering is recommended; it’s faster and cheaper.
  4. See it on screen whenever you like. Ask “show me the form” to open the viewer on the right. It shows the form as it stands at that moment — including your in-progress entry — but it doesn’t update live while you speak, so ask again after a batch of answers for a fresh view.
  5. Make corrections any time. Tell Benedict “change question 7 to no” or “the answer to age is 34.” He’ll overwrite the previous value.
  6. Skip PII when you’d rather not share it. If a question asks for a personal detail (patient name, date of birth, contact info) and you’d prefer to leave it blank, say “skip.” Benedict will leave that field blank and continue. The form will still submit.
  7. Submit when you’re ready. When all required fields are filled, Benedict will offer to submit. Say “yes please” or “submit” and he’ll email the finished paperwork (a PDF plus the original document format) to your practice’s registered address. He always double-checks with you before sending anything to a different address.

Speaking to Benedict — what works

  • Be natural. Whole sentences, fragments, “yeah” / “yep” / “no thanks” — all fine. He’s not a command line.
  • Batch your answers. “Yes to 1, 2 and 4; no to 3; the rest are all acknowledged.” → one save, less waiting. This is the biggest single thing you can do to speed up a session.
  • Use a question number OR a content cue. “Set question 12 to acknowledged” and “the one about anaesthetic should be local” both work. Use whichever feels more natural.
  • Type the fiddly bits. Email addresses, serial numbers, precise readings — typing them into the widget avoids mis-hearing and re-spelling, and costs nothing extra.
  • Ask for a review any time. “Review my entries” → he’ll read back everything you’ve filled so far.
  • Pause whenever you need to. Once the line has been quiet for a few seconds, the per-minute rate drops to a small fraction (currently about 5%) until you speak again — so thinking time is cheap. For a genuinely long break, say “save it, I’ll come back” and end the call; resuming later is seamless.
  • Don’t whisper. Speak clearly at normal volume; Benedict transcribes a clear signal better than a quiet one.

Resuming a form you didn’t finish

If you started a form earlier and didn’t finish, say “resume the Crown consent” or “show my in-progress forms.” Benedict will pick up where you left off — your previous answers are preserved. You don’t need to restate anything you’ve already answered.

Knowing where you stand

Benedict can answer four different “where am I?” questions — use the one you mean:

  • “What am I working on?” / “what’s outstanding?” — the forms you’ve started or not yet submitted.
  • “What haven’t I started yet?” — blank forms from your catalogue, grouped by type (audits, logs, risk assessments, policies…).
  • “What forms are available?” — the whole catalogue, grouped, with where each form stands.
  • “What’s due within a month?” — the due window (a week, a fortnight, a year — your choice).

Lists come back grouped and counted, ten names at a time — say “next” for the following page. For a bird’s-eye view on paper, say “email me a status report”: a PDF grouped by category goes to your practice’s registered address.

Surveys — let patients fill them in themselves

For the Patient Satisfaction Survey (and any survey-style form), the easiest and cheapest route is the web link. Say “email me the survey link” — Benedict sends a secure fill-in page link to your practice’s registered address. Forward it to patients, text it, or open it on a tablet at reception. Each web submission lands as one respondent in the same survey log — no voice time, works on any phone or computer, and is by far the cheapest way to collect responses.

You can also take responses by voice: Benedict asks for one respondent’s answers at a time (name first, or “anonymous”). Say “new respondent” to start the next one — each respondent becomes one row. Web and voice responses can be mixed freely in the same survey; submit the log whenever you’re ready.

Daily logs, autoclave records and cleaning schedules

For a daily record like an autoclave log, Benedict walks the day’s fields in order — and you can give the whole entry in one breath:

“Cycle one, yes, no, yellow, one three four, and the rest are all yes.”

Benedict maps your values onto the remaining fields in order and reads back exactly what he recorded. “The next three are yes” fills exactly three; “the rest are all yes” fills everything left that can safely take a yes — he’ll skip dates, initials and number readings and tell you which ones still need a real answer.

  • Fix a field in today’s entry: “change the minimum temperature to three” — or “clear the last two” to erase values from the draft.
  • Fix a field in an entry you’ve already saved: “in yesterday’s entry, change day sheets to no” — Benedict corrects that one field and reads back what changed.
  • Review any time: “read back today’s entry” mid-fill, and “show me the form” puts it on screen — today’s unsaved entry appears on the row marked IN PROGRESS.
  • Save before you submit: say “save the entry” when the day is done — Benedict reads the committed row back from the record. If you ask to submit while today’s entry is still unsaved, he’ll check with you first — a half-done entry is never silently dropped.
  • Machine details are remembered. Your autoclave’s type, serial number and location are asked once and carried forward every day — and questions that don’t apply to your equipment are skipped automatically.

Task-grid schedules (like the FR2 Surgery Cleaning Specification) work task by task — “Sinks and taps, done, today, initials SDA” — with the same shortcut: “today I did all of them” and Benedict confirms the list, then commits each one.

Long log, short day? Ask for “the log’s web link” — you’ll get a fill-in page just like the survey one. Each web submission adds one dated entry to the log, with no voice time at all.

Reviewing or correcting after submission

Submitted a form and noticed a typo? Just tell Benedict: “Open the last Crown consent and change question 27.” He’ll open a fresh editable copy with that change applied. You can make more changes — ask for as many as you need before saying “submit.” The original submission stays in the audit trail; the new copy supersedes it.

Every submission is also preserved as a permanent, tamper-evident record on IPFS (distributed storage) — you can view your submission audit record any time in the teckel App.

When you’re done revising, say “submit” explicitly. Benedict will NOT auto-submit just because you confirmed a single change — he waits for an explicit submission cue.

Ending the session

When you’re done, say “goodbye” or “that’s everything, thanks.” Benedict says one goodbye and ends the call himself — there’s no hang-up button to hunt for. Your progress is already saved as you go, so simply closing the page is fine too. Your total for the session is shown once the call ends.

Making Benedict yours — practice customisation (coming soon)

Every practice works a little differently, and the CQC expects your compliance paperwork to reflect your practice — not just a generic template. A customisation toolkit is rolling out on our roadmap:

  • Add your own questions to any standard form — and switch off non-mandatory standard questions that don’t apply to you. Regulator-standard questions always stay, and every change is recorded (who, what, when) so your paperwork remains inspection-ready.
  • Your letterhead on every submission — practice name, address, CQC registration number and logo added to the finished paperwork automatically.
  • Your branding on your portal — your logo and a browser-tab icon (favicon) for this site.
  • Attach evidence — upload inspection certificates, scans and photos against a form, straight from the computer where the files live.

Worth preparing now: have a good-quality copy of your practice logo to hand. For the browser-tab icon, if your practice doesn’t already have a favicon set, the free realfavicongenerator.net generates the complete set from your logo in a couple of minutes.

How Benedict charges you

Your bill runs on a single currency: teckel credits (tc). Every action Benedict takes against your data — reading your forms, writing an answer, opening the viewer, sending a submission, even just listening while you think — costs a small number of tc. When your session ends, that session’s total is shown at the top of the page, with the pound/pence equivalent.

There are no separate per-call fees or hidden charges. The main cost driver is simply conversation time — so the habits below (batching your answers, typing, and letting the web fill-in pages do the long repetitive work) are where the real savings live. One footnote: the call is billed by the minute, but sustained silence is billed at a small fraction (currently about 5%) of the rate — so pauses are cheap, though never free. For a long break, end the session rather than leave the line open; your progress keeps, and you can resume any time.

Submission emails and reports arrive from no_reply@teckel.io — add it to your safe senders so nothing lands in spam.

Topping up your tc balance — the teckel App

teckel handles ALL your billing through tc. To buy credit, install the teckel App on your phone or tablet:

Open the teckel App and navigate to the Account you use for Benedict, tap “Purchase teckel credits”, and choose a top-up amount. The purchase clears through the App’s in-app purchase flow (handled by Google or Apple, as appropriate). The credits land instantly in your teckel Account and are usable from any Benedict session immediately.

Benedict never stops mid-form for billing — your usage is totted up and drawn from your practice’s tc balance shortly after the work is done. If your balance is running low, Benedict will mention it once during a session — a gentle heads-up, never an interruption — so you can top up in the teckel App before it matters.

For specific pricing questions, contact teckel.io/support.

Six habits that keep costs down

1. Batch your answers when you can

Benedict can take a single sentence with many answers in it. Instead of saying “yes” · “yes” · “no” one at a time, try:

“Yes to questions 1, 2 and 4; no to question 3; the rest are all acknowledged.”

Benedict batch-fills them in one save instead of one round-trip per answer — and you’ll finish a 22-question form in about a minute instead of five. The same works on daily logs: “cycle one, yes, no, yellow, one three four — and the rest are all yes.”

You can do this at the start (give him the whole form), in the middle (skip ahead a block), or at the end (catch up the remaining ones).

2. Let the web page do the long ones

Surveys and long repetitive logs don’t need a conversation at all. Ask for “the survey link” or “the log’s web link” and fill the page on screen — or hand it to the person who has the answers. A web submission costs next to nothing compared with doing the same work by voice. This is the single biggest saving available.

3. Don’t ask to “see the form” more than once per state

When you ask Benedict to show you the form on screen, he mints a fresh view and the iframe loads it. If you then say “show me again” within the same minute, without any new data being entered, he’ll fire another full open-viewer cycle — which costs the same as the first time.

The right uses of “show me the form”:

  • At the start of a session, to see what you’re filling.
  • After you’ve filled several answers and want to verify them visually.
  • Just before you submit, as a final review.

If nothing’s changed since the last view, you can trust what was there.

4. Don’t repeat yourself if Benedict heard you the first time

If Benedict acknowledges an answer (“Got it — that’s a yes for question seven”), it’s recorded. You don’t need to confirm it again unless he asks you to. Each “yes I did say yes” is another roundtrip.

If you genuinely want to change an answer, just tell him plainly: “Change question seven to no.” He’ll update it once.

5. Pause freely — and step away properly for long breaks

A pause is cheap: once the line has been quiet for a few seconds, the per-minute rate drops by about 95% until you speak again. So think, check a record, take your time. For a genuinely long absence (a phone call, a patient), say “save it, I’ll come back later” and end the session — everything you’ve entered is preserved, and “resume the…” picks up exactly where you left off.

Short lookup — just pause. Long interruption — save and resume.

6. Submit only once

When all your non-PII answers are in, Benedict will offer to submit. Say yes (or “submit it” / “send it”) and let the submission complete. If you re-ask “is it sent yet?” five times, each one is a tool call.

You’ll know it’s sent when:

  • Benedict says the recipient has been emailed.
  • The form viewer shows the submitted state.

If you don’t hear that confirmation in 10–20 seconds, then it’s reasonable to ask once. Before that, just wait — submission is the longest single tool call (a PDF is generated, pinned to permanent storage, then emailed), so it can take a few seconds.

What’s already optimised for you

A few things the system handles automatically — you don’t need to do anything about them, but knowing they exist might save you a worried question:

  • PII fields can be left blank under GDPR. If a question asks for a patient name, date of birth, or anything else that identifies a person, you can say “skip” or simply not answer. Benedict will move on and the form will still submit cleanly.
  • What’s recorded is what’s read back. Every acknowledgement comes from the saved record itself — Benedict can’t invent fields, and he won’t call a form complete while required answers are blank; he names what’s missing instead.
  • Your in-progress entry is visible. On a daily log, the on-screen form shows today’s unsaved entry on the row marked IN PROGRESS — not saved yet, so what you see always matches where you are.
  • Emails only go where you’d expect. Submissions, reports and fill-in links go to your practice’s registered address; any other destination needs your explicit confirmation first.
  • The form viewer keeps itself alive. Its access link renews in the background, so a view you opened earlier keeps working — you very rarely need to ask to “reopen” it. (To see NEW answers, ask “show me the form” again — the view is a snapshot of the moment you asked.)
  • The cost display is simple and honest. Your total appears at the end of the session, always in the same currency at any scale: pence under £1, then pounds (with the residual pence still visible).
  • Confirmations are short. Benedict is trained to answer in one sentence when he can. If he’s giving you a long status report, it’s usually because you asked for one (“review my entries”, “what’s left”, etc.).

What costs the most (and how to spend it wisely)

Activity Relative cost Comment
Keeping the call open Low Sustained silence bills at about 5% of the per-minute rate — cheap, but end the call for long absences
Acknowledging a single answer Low A small fixed fee per action
Filling a single field Low–medium Bigger if the answer needs canonicalisation
Saving several answers in one go Medium Cheaper than the same answers individually
Opening the form viewer Medium Generates a fresh PDF preview
Submitting the form Highest PDF render + permanent storage + email
Generating a status report High Reads many records and produces a PDF

You’ll almost never see anything outside this range. If a session total comes in much higher than expected with no obvious cause, raise it at teckel.io/support — the usual cause is something asking Benedict to repeat itself.

When to ask for help

  • Benedict didn’t hear you correctly: repeat once, more slowly. He’ll auto-correct most homophones.
  • An answer was recorded wrong: “Change question N to X.” For a log entry you’ve already saved: “in yesterday’s entry, change day sheets to no.” Don’t try to undo the previous turn — just overwrite.
  • You’re stuck on a date format: Benedict will accept most natural phrasings (“ten May twenty-twenty-six”, “10th of May”, “10 May 2026”). If he keeps misinterpreting, spell out the year digits.
  • The form looks incomplete on screen: check whether the missing fields ask for personal details (PII) — those are allowed to stay blank under GDPR, and the form still submits.
  • Benedict says “I haven’t loaded a form yet”: tell him which form you want by name. He’ll find it in the catalogue.
  • Benedict says a form “isn’t set up yet”: that template really is incomplete on our side — it’s been flagged for the teckel team automatically. Pick another form in the meantime; nothing on your end is wrong.
  • Anything else: head to teckel.io/support — pricing, billing, account changes and anything Benedict can’t do himself are handled there.

A note on the session cost

The figure shown when a session ends is what that session cost. It does NOT show your account balance — check and top up your balance in the teckel App.

Costs scale with conversation length and form size — the six habits above are what keep a session lean. If a session comes in much higher than you expected, the most likely cause is repetition — re-read habits 1 and 4.
